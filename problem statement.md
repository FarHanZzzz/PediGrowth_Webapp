# Bangladesh Cerebral Palsy Problem Statement (User Perspective)

## Purpose of this document
This document provides a user-centered, scientifically backed problem statement for cerebral palsy (CP) in Bangladesh, with an evidence audit of key claims.

## User perspective: what the problem feels like on the ground
For many families in rural Bangladesh, the journey starts with concern, not diagnosis. A child is not meeting developmental milestones, but the caregiver has limited access to specialist evaluation, limited money for repeated travel, and limited local rehabilitation options. Delays are common, so care starts late, costs rise, and functional opportunities are lost during the critical early years.

For clinicians and frontline workers, demand is high but capacity is thin. Service points are concentrated in urban settings, multidisciplinary teams are scarce, and most care is paid out-of-pocket. This creates a mismatch: children need coordinated, early, ongoing intervention, but the system often provides fragmented, delayed, and financially burdensome care.

## Evidence audit (Bangladesh-specific)

| Claim | Evidence status | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| CP burden in Bangladesh is high. | Strong | Population-based surveillance estimated CP prevalence at 3.4 per 1000 children (95% CI 3.2-3.7), approximately 233,514 children nationally (95% CI 219,778-254,118). PMID: 30394528, DOI: 10.1111/dmcn.14013 |
| Diagnosis is delayed in routine conditions. | Strong | Mean age at CP diagnosis reported as 5 years 2 months in a Bangladesh population-based cohort. PMID: 30394528 |
| Large portions of children do not access rehabilitation. | Strong | In one Bangladesh population cohort, 78.2% had never received rehabilitation (PMID: 30394528). In a larger later BCPR cohort, 49.8% had never received rehabilitation (PMID: 33939721). |
| Rehabilitation often starts late. | Strong | Mean age at rehabilitation commencement was 3 years 10 months; 30.4% first started at or after 5 years. PMID: 33939721, DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0250640 |
| Functional and comorbidity burden is high. | Strong | In rural Bangladesh BCPR data (n=3820), 81% had at least one associated impairment: hearing 18%, speech 74%, intellectual 40%, visual 14%, epilepsy 33%. PMID: 36836130, DOI: 10.3390/jcm12041597 |
| Education access is very limited for many affected children. | Strong | Among school-aged children with associated impairments, mainstream/special school enrollment was low (for example: hearing impairment 6.2%, speech impairment 9.7%, visual impairment 7.5%, intellectual impairment 12%, epilepsy 9.2%). PMID: 36836130 |
| System capacity is structurally constrained. | Strong | Bangladesh has 6.8 rehabilitation units per 1 million people; only 6.2% are in rural areas; 66.3% require out-of-pocket payment. Workforce per 1 million: 9.4 physiotherapists, 1.3 occupational therapists, 0.9 speech and language therapists, 0.2 prosthetist/orthotists. PMID: 34176400, DOI: 10.1080/09638288.2021.1939799 |
| Service delivery redesign can improve access and timeliness. | Strong | SMART-CP cluster RCT (n=968) in rural Bangladesh increased rehabilitation uptake (99.4% intervention vs 68.2% control at endline), reduced age at diagnosis (2.0 vs 3.8 years), reduced age at rehab start (1.8 vs 3.6 years), lowered travel burden (18.1 km vs 113.4 km), and lowered out-of-pocket spending ($1.5 vs $2.9 per month). PMID: 40193125, DOI: 10.1001/jamapediatrics.2025.0150 |
| Earlier detection is feasible in Bangladesh if best-practice tools are used. | Strong | In a high-risk NICU cohort, CP was accurately diagnosable as early as 3 months with GMA+HINE (sensitivity 0.91, specificity 1.00). PMID: 41052810, DOI: 10.1136/bmjpo-2025-003921 |

## Synthesis: core user problem
The Bangladesh CP problem is not only prevalence. It is a timing-access-equity failure:

1. Timing failure: diagnosis and rehabilitation often happen after critical early neurodevelopment windows.
2. Access failure: services are geographically concentrated, workforce-limited, and not consistently multidisciplinary.
3. Equity failure: households with lower income and lower parental education face higher risk of non-use, while out-of-pocket financing remains dominant.

## Formal problem statement
Children with cerebral palsy in Bangladesh, especially in rural and low-income households, are frequently identified and linked to rehabilitation too late because the current care pathway depends on scarce specialist capacity, urban-centered services, and high out-of-pocket spending. This delay and fragmentation contribute to preventable loss of developmental opportunity, high unmanaged associated impairments, poor school participation, and sustained caregiver burden.

## Product-relevant gap statement (non-diagnostic positioning)
Bangladesh needs scalable, low-cost, workflow-compatible tools that help frontline services and caregivers:

1. Identify risk earlier and route children to appropriate clinical assessment.
2. Reduce time-to-referral and time-to-rehabilitation initiation.
3. Support continuity of follow-up where specialist access is sparse.
4. Generate clear, explainable outputs that assist clinical decision support, not replace diagnosis.

## Scope note
This statement supports early identification and referral optimization. It does not position digital screening as a standalone diagnostic substitute for clinician-led CP diagnosis.
